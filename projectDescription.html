Overview

Topic

ToDoTopia is a web-based, games platform that links into companies’ productivity tools to determine in-game XP. Being a web-based application allows for more flexibility as it is easier to release updates and users are not required to install an application on their personal computer. Our original idea was to create a productivity tool that possessed gamification features, but after some research we decided to focus on making a more engaging gaming experience that links into already popular productivity tools, rather than focusing on building a productivity tool. Our goal for this platform is to create a more fun and competitive work environment by turning completed tasks into XP that can be used to buy troops, guns and other equipment in a real-time strategy (RTS) game. We want to encourage a time at the end of each week or sprint for employees to sit down, have a drink and verse each other in a battle that is highly influenced by the level of productivity of each individual during that week or sprint.

To link into productivity tools used by our customers (such as Jira and Azure DevOps) will require the use of an API. We will use this API to send across information related to the task such as its difficulty, size and who completes it. In ToDoTopia’s game, XP will be used to purchase troops, guns, tanks, etc. ToDoTopia will use the information received about the task to translate it to in-game XP. The task’s size will be used to determine how much XP the user will receive and the employee who completes the task or moves it to the next stage of progress will be awarded this XP. The overall outcome of this project is to create a web-based game’s platform that uses data from a company’s task manager (e.g., Jira or Azure DevOps) to allow employees to gain in-game XP by completing more tasks.

Motivation

Gamification of tasks is not necessarily a new idea. Companies such as Habitica have been doing it for a while now in a way of making your daily tasks and activities rewarding by giving you a character who starts at level one and has no items. The idea is that you self-appoint yourself tasks and as you complete those tasks you are rewarded with better equipment to help your character progress. However, these applications are more on an individual basis and the game play is limited. Keeping a team motivated during a project can be difficult, but gamification has shown to keep the attention of employees and increase their productivity. What is gamification? Gamification at its core is turning work into play. There is a model known as Octalysis that explains in detail the eight core drives that gamify something whether it be a task at work or a video game.

These eight core drives are as listed below:

  • Epic Meaning and calling: “When a player believes that he is doing something greater than himself.” (Yu-kai Chou: Gamification & Behavioral Design)
  • Development and Accomplishment: “The internal drive of making progress, developing skills, and eventually overcoming challenges.” (Yu-kai Chou: Gamification & Behavioral Design)
  • Empowerment and Creativity: “When users are engaged in a creative process where they have to repeatedly figure things out and try different combinations.” (Yu-kai Chou: Gamification & Behavioral Design)
  • Ownership and Possession: “Where users are motivated because they feel like they own something. When a player feels ownership, she innately wants to make what she owns better and own even more.” (Yu-kai Chou: Gamification & Behavioral Design)
  • Social Influence and Relatedness: ‘’This drive incorporates all the social elements that drive people, including: mentorship, acceptance, social responses, companionship, as well as competition and envy. When you see a friend that is amazing at some skill or owns something extraordinary, you become driven to reach the same level” (Yu-kai Chou: Gamification & Behavioral Design)
  • Scarcity and Impatience: “The drive of wanting something because you can’t have it. Many games have Appointment Dynamics (come back 2 hours later to get your reward) – the fact that people can’t get something right now motivates them to think about it all day long.” (Yu-kai Chou: Gamification & Behavioral Design)
  • Unpredictability: “Generally, this is a harmless drive of wanting to find out what will happen next. If you don’t know what’s going to happen, your brain is engaged, and you think about it often. Many people watch movies or read novels because of this drive. However, this drive is also the primary factor behind gambling addiction.” (Yu-kai Chou: Gamification & Behavioral Design)
  • Loss and Avoidance: “Based upon the avoidance of something negative happening.” (Yu-kai Chou: Gamification & Behavioral Design)

Every good game consists of these eight properties, and they can be used to motivate people and keep them focused. This is the motivation for ToDoTopia, to use gamification to turn completing tasks in a Jira or Azure DevOps Kanban board into a game.

Landscape

The idea of gamifying tasks is already out there, and applications have been made that use this. Habitica is a habit tracking and task management app. It gamifies completing tasks and practicing habits by assigning tasks with gold. This gold is used to build up an avatar and a team can “battle” monsters by completing more tasks and habits. The “battle” in Habitica, however, does not require any gameplay, rather every day the monster is damaged until its health runs out. Other apps such as Todoist and Forest incorporate gamification into their app designs. Todoist is a well-known task manager for individuals and teams. It possesses gamification in the form of “Karma” and “Karma Levels”. As tasks are complete, the user obtains “Karma”, a form of XP or points, that builds up levels. Although Todoist is gamified, it doesn’t possess any true gameplay. Forest is a time-tracking application built for setting a timer when working on a particular task. Its gamification is in the form of an animated forest where each tree is a timer session. When a timer is completed, a tree is grown in the forest, but when the timer is stopped halfway through the session a dead tree is added to the forest. What ToDoTopia is offering that is different from all these apps, and any other application made with a similar purpose, is gameplay. ToDoTopia itself isn’t a task manager, or a Kanban board, but a web-based gaming platform allowing for actual gameplay. Actual gameplay is far more engaging than adding armour to an avatar or growing trees in a forest. Since our focus is on gameplay, we can create better games that link into already great productivity tools.

What the Web App Will Look Like

Home Page

The web app of ToDoTopia, which will be accessed through the domain todotopia.com, is the place users will set up their accounts, deal with XP, check leaderboards and open games. These games will be hosted separately, but will link to the web app. The home page of ToDoTopia, once the user is signed in, will display the team they are in, their recent activity in the companies chosen Work-In-Progress platform (starting with Azure DevOps), their current XP, an up-to-date leaderboard for the team and company and a menu that allows the user to navigate the web app. At its core, the ToDoTopia web app home page will be an overview of the user’s current statistics and activity in their WIP application. Users will not be able to edit or complete tasks from ToDoTopia, but will be able to view the tasks and their relevant properties.

Dashboard

The dashboard will be opened from the home page of ToDoTopia’s web app via the menu and will display the user’s recent activity in greater detail and the user’s current XP. In the dashboard, the user will be able to assign XP to their game of choice as there will be multiple games in the future of ToDoTopia. However, in the first version of ToDoTopia there will be only one game and this step of assigning XP to a chosen game will be automated. When the user decides to use their XP to purchase in-game items (such as troops, tanks, planes, etc.), they will be required to open the game via the games menu from the home page of the web app.

Leaderboard

The home page will display the leaderboard of the user’s team. When opened, the user will be able interact with leaderboard items. These items are the users and their current XP. The user will also be able to filter the leaderboard by their team and company, and possibly teams in a specific department of the company if the company has set up their account in such a way. IT teams will be displayed in the leaderboard as well as individuals. This will allow the company to check on how teams are performing against each other. The leaderboard of the web app will only be sorted with the data from the WIP Kanban board and not the games. If the user would like to view how each member is performing in a game, they will be required to open the games menu and click on the specific game’s leaderboard. This will display the leaderboard for that game based on battles won, XP lost, etc.

Admin

Admin will be a page of ToDoTopia only accessible to the team leader who created the team account. Other team members will not have the option to view this page. The admin page will be where the team leader can set defaults for how XP will be assigned. They will be able to enter an amount of XP per story point. Some teams will use 5 story points, and others might use 10, so the web app will be developed to incorporate the team’s style. An example could look like this: 1 SP (story point) = 10 XP, 2 SP = 25 XP, 3 SP = 50XP, 4 SP = 100 XP, and 5 SP = 200 XP. These numbers will be different for every team and can be changed at any time only by the team leader.

In the Admin page, the team leader will also be able to modify the cost of in-game items. The user will be required to select the game, then the items in that game will be listed with the corresponding XP required to purchase that item. The team leader will be able to enter a new amount per item at any time, allowing for the cost of troops, tanks, planes, etc. to be based on the team’s story points style.

How the Web App will be Built

.NET Framework

There are dozens of frameworks we can choose from to create the web app and games. Our games will be developed using Unity game engine and the language C#. Our web app will be developed using ASP.NET Core which is a framework that uses the languages of HTML, CSS, JavaScript and C# to programme a web app. We will be required to learn these languages and have already started on this journey as we have used HTML, CSS and a bit of JavaScript to create a simple website hosted on GitHub. Razor will also be used and is any file with the suffix .cshtml. Razor is a server-side markup language that is a combination of HTML and C#. ASP.NET Core also allows for the creation of APIs which will be needed to transmit data from the web app to the games and vice versa.

Connecting to Azure DevOps (and Jira) through API

ToDoTopia’s web app will constantly be ‘communicating’ with Azure DevOps in the backend on the server side by sending a get request through an API to the DevOps Kanban board used by the team. Data will be returned to the web app and entered into its databases. If tasks are completed, then that data will run through some functions which will be programmed to calculate the amount of XP to be assigned and who to assign it to. All this will happen in the backend on the server side and the results will be displayed in the user’s browser. The web app itself will receive updates about XP using AJAX (Asynchronous JavaScript and XML) with the data in the format of JSON (JavaScript Object Notation). When tasks are added in the DevOps Kanban board, they will be sent to ToDoTopia’s databases through an API. Before being saved to the database, a programme will run, turning the storypoints from the task into XP based on the team leader’s settings in Admin. This data will sit in the database until the task is completed or moved into a new stage of progression (In Progress, Ready for Review, Complete, etc.). When these changes are made to the task in the Kanban board, the XP will be assigned to the user who changed it and saved to a new database. If a task is moved back in a stage of progression (e.g. Complete to In Progress), that XP will be subtracted from the user’s ToDoTopia account and moved back to the unassigned XP database.

Connecting to Game through API (to transfer XP data)

ToDoTopia’s games will be hosted separate from the web app and will receive data about XP via an API. At the start of a match/battle in a game, the game will send a final get request to the web app before freezing XP updates to the game. This freezing allows users to purchase any items and play the game without any interference from tasks being changed in the DevOps Kanban board. After the match/battle, the game will update XP data in the databases based on the results of the match/battle. Any XP that was used to purchase in-game items will be subtracted from the user’s total XP and any XP won in-game will be added to the user’s XP and be viewable in the web app.

How does the game save data and keep all data up to date?

ToDoTopia will be a web-based game that will use a cloud service (AWS/Azure) to manage all of our in-game data requirements, allowing you to view all of your game data from any device and from anywhere.

Cloud saves is a feature that allows games and platforms that require saving game files to use cloud storage. This feature covers everything from in-game progress storage to settings, purchases, and profiles. In-game purchases are a typical feature of today's digital gaming, and they demand payment using game credits, which are frequently purchased with real money. Because the data is saved on the cloud, the account information provided for this purpose is protected from being erased or breached.

When a gaming session finishes, cloud saves are an automated process that synchronises the saved data from the game to cloud storage. To prevent gaming frauds cloud, the player is required to manually upload the locally saved game data to the ui if there is an issue during this sync. Another alternative is to resume the sync by downloading previously saved game data from the cloud.

Benefits of Cloud saves

With huge volumes of data being kept on the cloud, the ability to save and synchronise data through cloud storage has become a requirement in our life. Although the free space in smartphones, computers, and gaming consoles makes customers happy, the benefits of cloud storage disappear when the service experiences technical difficulties or fails to function effectively.

For instances where the gamer wants to play alone without connecting to servers for multiplayer mode, the sync will simply not happen. Also, when they want to show off their in-game achievements to a friend and the location doesn’t offer sufficient internet, the game progress that is indicated might not be completely updated. For these reasons, cloud saves come in handy when accessing games through different platforms, internet speeds, and locations.

Project story

How it Began:

The inception of ToDoTopia began with Jesse Watson’s first assignment for RMIT’s Introductory IT course.

In an independent project he researched the viability and real-world applications of gamification and how it can be used to increase productivity and maintaining a higher level of motivation to carry out tasks. The concept comes from visualizing a fusion between gamification to-do lists like Habitica which is an individual centric gamified to-do list application, and Todoist a more group centric application with a professional UI. The idea at this stage was to have the completed tasks award points which would subsequently display as a village or character which then acquires better equipment, or the town’s size and quality would improve.

This concept became the idea with which we as Team Thirteen from RMIT chose to further develop in the endeavour of creating a product which would fill an unchartered territory which is gamification in a work environment.

How it has Progressed:

Having realised that the concept of gamifying a to-do list for individuals was a well traversed territory we subsequently opted to venture into an untapped market which is workplace gamification. After discussing the goals for project ToDoTopia, we at Team Thirteen decided that we would like to design a game that linked workplace assigned tasks to a rewards system that would allow users to accumulate points that are distributed on completion of tasks, the number of points distributed will be controlled by the organisations designated administrators. Organisations that use ToDoTopia will have the ability to customize many aspects of the game and its web application to further personalize it to their requirements and preferences.

We settled upon using Unity as the engine for the game, as it has a wide range of tools which will assist us greatly in building our game. From research on the game engine Unity, we found that to begin developing with it is free until revenue and funding exceeds 100k USD as described on their website, therefore we don’t need to worry about buying the software until we start to make a profit from the game. As we develop the game further, we may find limitations in the free Unity package and may need to consider purchasing one of the premium options.

The internet would be used to host our Unity game, whilst utilising cloud storage for both the HTML page and Unity game. Our goal is to have a web application serve as the core for the project ToDoTopia. While the web app will be where the tasks are completed for points and the game portion will be where the rewards are utilised in gameplay, we hope to expand on the number of ways you can use rewards by adding new games linked to the rewards system, establishing a connection with pre-existing rewards systems like Flybuy or Woolworths points for example, or even offering company perks or bonuses based on ranking in a company leaderboard.

The task system would be deployed on an Azure DevOps Kanban board system and connected via API. Team Thirteen doesn’t have a lot of experience using Azure DevOps, Kanban boards, or APIs so more research will need to be undertaken to finalise this plan, we may find we can even implement features for Scrum boards and other niche boards. At this stage we have decided to pursue using a Kanban board hosted with Azure DevOps Boards which will connect to the game via an API service like Postman to allow them both to interconnect their experience points and rewards systems. Conclusion: ToDoTopia has undergone significant improvements since its conception, though a lot of planning and learning is still required before we can make a start on developing ToDoTopia into the product that we have envisioned.

What Stage are we at with the Project:

We have laid out the framework for our development and learning plan for building ToDoTopia, with a large emphasis being placed upon the learning path and prioritisation of skills we will need for development.

In consideration of the fact that many of us in Team Thirteen lack the technical skills and knowledge to undertake the building of the game in Unity, we have instead focused our attention on planning the games features, the tools we need to generate these features, any software infrastructure required, also any skills and talents we will need to develop.

We’ve created concept pieces for the game’s UI and described what the finished product will look like along with the modes we will first develop for the game. We aim to release game modes individually to maintain a minimum viable product through development and expand upon our offered services over time. We’ve discussed using a subscription fee model as the games form of monetization, subscription fees will allow us to charge a modest sum during our early stages of development where our offering of game modes may be meagre. As our services diversify, we can always raise our subscription fee, this eliminates any lost revenue from selling a one-time purchase game license for a low price then adding a multitude of features then raising the price.

Due to our deficiencies in software development and game design we have not made substantial progress in the material development of the ToDoTopia’s game or web application components, instead we have only created concepts and elaborated on our plan to build the project.

Any Dead Ends we have come Across:

There are a few areas in which we are lacking such as our programming, artistic, and sound editing abilities. Listed here is just a few of our shortcomings but are our most critical issues or issues without simple resolutions.

Our programming ability is largely rudimentary at this stage which prevents us from creating a game with our own code at this stage. To overcome this deficit, we will need to dedicate time to learning programming languages like C#, C++, or JavaScript for our required applications for use with Unity and its toolkits we intend to use. Over time, as we learn more about programming and Unity, we might discover applications that are more suitable to our needs and thus may need to learn to use any new software and languages as well.

To learn these programming languages there are many excellent free resources available online or we could opt to enrol in a paid bootcamp for learning to code. One of the free resources available are W3Schools which provides learning materials for JavaScript and many of the most popular programming languages such as C#, C++, Python and more. Then secondly there is FreeCodeCamp which presently offers 12 free certification courses to choose from, ranging from Back-End Development and APIs to Scientific Computing with Python to name a couple. Utilising free learning resources and development software will help us greatly along the way as it will likely be a long road towards making ToDoTopia into a profitable service.

Artistic talent is another area in which we lack, Team Thirteen was formed from students enrolled in an Introductory IT course so naturally the chances of it being a field of experience was slim.

We will need to develop the art related aspects of both our web application and the game itself. For the web application we will need to learn more about UI & UX design to increase user satisfaction with our products, this design could be undertaken by any of us after having studied UI & UX design either at RMIT, FreeCodeCamp provides a course on Front-End Development which could be a useful resource for one or more of us to study to ensure a smooth user interaction with our web application.

We will also need someone with the ability to create concept art, game art, animations, and visual effects to name just a few. These are skills we absolutely do not have presently and therefore cannot develop the art of the game unless we were to use premade resources provided within Unity, or one of Team Thirteens members chose to specialise in artistic design, or we recruit outside help from someone with art design skills.

James Costello has shown an aptitude for using Adobe Photoshop to produce visual elements used in the creation of our assessment webpages, UI & UX may be an avenue James wishes to further his skills in for the project.

Hayden Bleys also has qualifications in Visual Arts, which may serve as a starting point to further his education in Art Design for games and become a Technical Artist.

Reflecting upon our shortcomings in the art design area allows us to see that we need someone to be take on learning so that they can specialise in UI & UX as it is one of the most critical aspects. We also now know that one issue we will need to address before we advance with the production of ToDoTopia is our lack of Art Design skills.
